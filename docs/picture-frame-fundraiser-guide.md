## Part 2: Building the Frame

### Step 1: Calculate Your Dimensions
**Formula:** Frame opening = Artwork size + ⅛" clearance on each side

For an 8" x 10" artwork:
- Frame opening = 8⅛" x 10⅛"
- Add twice the width of your wood strip to get outer dimensions

### Step 2: Cutting the Wood

1. **Set your miter saw to 45 degrees**
2. **Measure carefully** - measure twice, cut once
3. **Cut four pieces:**
   - Two pieces for the longer sides (length of opening + 2x wood width)
   - Two pieces for the shorter sides (width of opening + 2x wood width)

**Pro Tip:** Cut all your 45° angles pointing the same direction, then flip the wood for the opposite angle. This ensures consistent corners.

### Step 3: Cut the Rabbet (Groove for Glass/Art)

The rabbet is a groove on the back of the frame that holds the glass, mat, artwork, and backing.

**Option A - Router method:**
1. Use a rabbet bit in a router
2. Cut a groove ⅜" wide x ⅜" deep on the back inner edge

**Option B - Table saw method:**
1. Make two passes - one flat, one on edge
2. Create a ⅜" x ⅜" step

**Option C - No power tools:**
1. Buy pre-rabbeted frame molding from a craft store
2. Or use thin strips of wood glued to the back to create the lip

### Step 4: Assemble the Frame

1. **Dry fit first** - Put all pieces together without glue to check fit
2. **Apply wood glue** to both surfaces of each miter joint
3. **Clamp the frame:**
   - Use corner clamps on each corner, OR
   - Use a band clamp around the entire frame
4. **Wipe excess glue** with a damp cloth immediately
5. **Let dry 24 hours** minimum

### Step 5: Reinforce Corners (Optional but Recommended)

For extra strength:
- **Corrugated fasteners:** Hammer into back of corners
- **Dowels:** Drill and insert glued dowels through corners
- **V-nails/wedges:** Use a specialized framing tool

### Step 6: Sand the Frame

1. Start with **120 grit** sandpaper
2. Sand with the grain, not against
3. Round edges slightly for a professional look
4. Finish with **220 grit** for smoothness
5. Wipe with a tack cloth to remove dust

### Step 7: Finish the Frame

**For Stained Finish:**
1. Apply wood conditioner (especially for pine/softwood)
2. Apply stain with foam brush, following the grain
3. Wipe off excess after 5-10 minutes
4. Let dry 24 hours
5. Apply 2-3 coats of polyurethane, sanding lightly between coats

**For Painted Finish:**
1. Apply primer if using light colors
2. Apply 2-3 thin coats of paint (chalk paint works great)
3. Let dry between coats
4. Optional: distress edges with sandpaper for rustic look
5. Seal with clear coat or wax

## Part 3: Protecting the Artwork

### Understanding Art Protection

**Three enemies of artwork:**
1. **UV Light** - Fades colors over time
2. **Acid** - Causes yellowing and deterioration
3. **Moisture** - Causes mold, warping, staining

### Choosing Your Glazing (Front Protection)

| Type | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|------|------|------|----------|
| Regular glass | Cheap, clear, scratch-resistant | Heavy, breakable, no UV protection | Budget frames |
| UV-filtering glass | Blocks 99% UV | Expensive, still breakable | Valuable art |
| Regular acrylic | Lightweight, shatter-resistant | Scratches easily | Kids' art, shipping |
| UV-filtering acrylic | Light, UV protection | More expensive | Quality protection |
| Museum glass | Anti-glare + UV | Very expensive | Professional work |

**For fundraisers:** Regular glass or regular acrylic is cost-effective.

### Matting the Artwork

Mats serve two purposes:
1. **Aesthetic** - Creates visual separation and frames the art
2. **Protection** - Keeps artwork from touching the glass (prevents moisture damage)

**How to cut a mat:**
1. Mark the window opening on the back of the mat board
2. Use a mat cutter or utility knife with straightedge
3. Cut with blade angled at 45° for beveled edge
4. Standard mat width: 2-3 inches on all sides

**CRITICAL:** Use only **acid-free** mat board. Regular cardboard will yellow and damage artwork.

### Mounting the Artwork

**NEVER use regular tape, rubber cement, or spray adhesive on original artwork!**

**Safe mounting methods:**

1. **Photo corners** (safest)
   - Small triangular pockets that hold corners
   - Artwork can be removed without damage

2. **Acid-free hinge tape**
   - Japanese tissue + wheat starch paste
   - Or archival linen tape
   - Apply only at top edge so art hangs

3. **Mounting corners**
   - Self-adhesive pockets
   - Quick and easy for fundraisers

### Backing the Frame

1. Use **acid-free foam board** or **acid-free corrugated board**
2. Cut to fit snugly in the rabbet
3. This protects from dust, pests, and physical damage from behind

### Creating a Sealed Package (Recommended!)

For maximum protection, seal the artwork between glass and backing BEFORE putting it in the frame:

**Why Seal?**
- Prevents moisture from reaching the artwork
- Keeps out dust, insects, and pollutants
- Creates a microenvironment that protects the art
- Professional museums use this technique

**How to Create a Sealed Package:**

1. **Cut your glass/acrylic** to size and clean thoroughly
2. **Stack in order:**
   - Glass (front)
   - Mat board (optional, for spacing)
   - Artwork (mounted to backing or mat)
   - Acid-free backing board
3. **Align all edges** so the stack is perfectly even
4. **Apply framing tape around ALL edges:**
   - Use **acid-free framing tape** or **archival sealing tape**
   - Popular options: Lineco Frame Sealing Tape, Filmoplast P90
   - Wrap tape around the edge, covering both front glass and back board
   - Overlap tape at corners
   - Press firmly to create an airtight seal

### ⚠️ Water Protection Levels - Be Honest!

| Protection Level | Method | Protects Against |
|-----------------|--------|------------------|
| **Basic** | Framing tape only | Dust, insects, humidity changes, air pollutants |
| **Good** | Framing tape + Marvelseal backing | Above + high humidity environments |
| **Better** | Silicone caulk around edges | Above + splashes, brief water contact |
| **Best** | Full waterproof seal (see below) | Submersion, floods, heavy rain |

**Standard framing tape is NOT waterproof!** It handles normal humidity but won't survive:
- Water spills directly on frame
- Hanging in bathrooms/kitchens with steam
- Outdoor display
- Flooding

### For TRUE Waterproofing (If Needed)

If frames will be in wet environments or you want maximum protection:

**⚠️ IMPORTANT: Standard backing is NOT waterproof!**
- Foam board, cardboard, mat board all ABSORB water
- Even with sealed edges, water finds a way in

**Step 1: Use Waterproof Backing**
| Material | Pros | Cons | Cost |
|----------|------|------|------|
| **Coroplast** (corrugated plastic) | Cheap, light, easy to cut | Looks cheap | $5-10/sheet |
| **Sintra/PVC foam board** | Smooth, rigid, professional | Harder to cut | $10-20/sheet |
| **Acrylic sheet** | Clear option, very rigid | Heavy, expensive | $15-30 |
| **Gatorboard** | Foam core + plastic skin | Water resistant, not proof | $10-15/sheet |

### 🌿 Eco-Friendly Waterproof Options

If sustainability matters to you or your buyers:

**Natural & Traditional Backing Materials:**

These are truly natural materials that can be made water-resistant with proper treatment:

| Material | Source | Water Protection | Cost | Notes |
|----------|--------|------------------|------|-------|
| **Birch plywood** (⅛" thin) | Wood, renewable | ✅ With sealant | $8-15/sheet | Beautiful grain, lightweight |
| **Hardboard/Masonite** | Compressed wood fibers | ✅ With sealant | $5-10/sheet | Smooth, dense, traditional |
| **Baltic birch plywood** | Sustainably harvested | ✅ With sealant | $10-15/sheet | Premium, strong, no voids |
| **Cork board** (⅛"-¼") | Cork oak bark (renewable) | ⚠️ With heavy sealing | $10-20/sheet | Sustainable, grows back |
| **Wheat straw board** | Agricultural waste | ⚠️ With sealant | $8-12/sheet | Upcycled, eco-friendly |
| **Museum board** (thick) | Cotton/alpha cellulose | ❌ Not waterproof | $15-25/sheet | Archival, for dry indoor only |

**How to Waterproof Natural Wood Backing:**

1. **Start with thin wood** (⅛" birch plywood is perfect)
2. **Choose your natural sealant:**

**Best Natural Sealants for Wood:**
| Sealant | Application | Waterproof Level | Dry Time | Notes |
|---------|-------------|------------------|----------|-------|
| **Tung oil** (pure) | 3-4 thin coats | ✅ Excellent | 24hr/coat | Food-safe, beautiful finish |
| **Linseed oil** (boiled) | 2-3 coats | ✅ Good | 24hr/coat | Traditional, amber tone |
| **Danish oil** | 2-3 coats | ✅ Good | 8-12hr/coat | Mix of oils, easy to apply |
| **Shellac** | 2-3 coats | ✅ Excellent | 1-2hr/coat | Natural resin, fast drying |
| **Beeswax + oil mix** | Multiple coats | ⚠️ Moderate | 2hr/coat | Beautiful, needs reapplying |
| **Spar varnish** (marine) | 2 coats | ✅ Excellent | 24hr/coat | For outdoor/high moisture |

**Step-by-Step Natural Wood Backing:**

1. **Cut ⅛" birch plywood** to size
2. **Sand smooth** with 220 grit
3. **Apply tung oil or shellac:**
   - Tung oil: Brush on thin coat, wipe excess after 15 min, repeat 3x
   - Shellac: Brush on, let dry 2 hours, light sand, repeat 2-3x
4. **Let cure 48 hours** before assembly
5. **Result:** Beautiful natural wood back that's waterproof!

**DIY Beeswax Edge Seal:**
1. Get beeswax pellets or a block (~$10 for lots of frames)
2. Melt in double boiler or microwave (low heat)
3. Brush melted wax around all edges while warm
4. Let cool and harden (2-3 minutes)
5. Buff with soft cloth for smooth finish
6. Creates natural waterproof seal!

**Where to Find Eco Materials:**
- **Recycled HDPE boards**: Hardware stores (deck boards), Amazon "recycled plastic sheet"
- **Beeswax**: Craft stores, farmer's markets, Amazon
- **Tung oil**: Hardware stores (in wood finish aisle), "pure tung oil"
- **Bamboo boards**: Craft stores, lumber yards
- **Aluminum sheet**: Hardware stores, metal suppliers (thin gauge is easy to cut)

**Step 2: Waterproof Edge Seal**

**Option 1: Silicone Edge Seal**
1. Use waterproof backing (Coroplast or Sintra)
2. Stack: Glass → Mat → Art → Waterproof backing
3. Run a thin bead of **clear silicone caulk** around ALL edges
4. Smooth with finger dipped in soapy water
5. Let cure 24 hours before handling
6. Silicone is waterproof and flexible

**Option 2: Butyl Tape (Easiest)**
1. Use waterproof backing
2. Seal edges with **butyl tape** (used for RV/boat sealing)
3. Butyl is sticky, waterproof, and stays flexible
4. Found in hardware stores (roofing/RV section)

**Option 3: Heat-Shrink Film**
1. Use **document laminating pouches** (NOT regular lamination - it damages art over time)
2. Or **UV-protective heat-shrink film** designed for archival use
3. Seals completely around the package

**Option 4: Resin Coating (Permanent)**
1. Pour **clear epoxy resin** over the front of artwork
2. Creates glass-like waterproof surface
3. ⚠️ PERMANENT - cannot be undone
4. Best for prints, NOT original art

### Complete Waterproof Assembly

For TRUE waterproof protection, you need BOTH:
1. ✅ Waterproof backing (plastic, not cardboard/foam)
2. ✅ Waterproof edge seal (silicone or butyl tape)

```
WATERPROOF PACKAGE:
┌─────────────────────┐
│   Glass/Acrylic     │ ← Waterproof
├─────────────────────┤
│   Mat (optional)    │ ← Will get wet if seal fails
├─────────────────────┤
│   Artwork           │ ← Protected in center
├─────────────────────┤
│ Coroplast/Sintra    │ ← Waterproof backing
└─────────────────────┘
        ↑
   Silicone or butyl tape
   sealing ALL edges
```

**Best Practice for Fundraiser Frames:**
- Standard acid-free tape seal is fine for normal indoor use
- Add silicone edge seal if you want water resistance
- Use waterproof backing + silicone for full waterproofing
- Be clear with buyers about protection level

**Tape Options:**
| Type | Waterproof? | Use Case |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Lineco Frame Sealing Tape | ❌ No | Dust, humidity, archival |
| Filmoplast P90 | ❌ No | Archival, reversible |
| Aluminum Foil Tape | ⚠️ Resistant | Moisture barrier on back |
| Butyl Tape | ✅ Yes | Full waterproof seal |
| Clear Silicone Caulk | ✅ Yes | Waterproof edges |

**Pro Tips:**
- The sealed package then drops into your frame as one unit
- If using a mat, the mat can be part of the sealed package
- For extra moisture protection, add a sheet of **Marvelseal** (aluminum barrier film) behind the backing before sealing
- Label the back with pencil: date sealed, materials used

## Part 4: Final Assembly

### Step-by-Step Assembly

1. **Clean the glass thoroughly** - Use glass cleaner, ensure no fingerprints
2. **Layer in order from front to back:**
   - Glass
   - Mat (if using)
   - Artwork
   - Acid-free backing board
3. **Secure with glazier points:**
   - Use a glazier point driver or flat-head screwdriver
   - Push points into frame every 3-4 inches
4. **Alternative: Turn buttons** for easy opening later

### Sealing the Back (Two Methods)

**Method 1: Sealed Package (Best Protection)**
Seal art between glass and backing BEFORE putting in frame:
1. Stack: Glass → Mat → Artwork → Backing
2. Wrap **acid-free framing tape** around ALL four edges
3. Tape should cover edge of glass AND backing (like wrapping a gift edge)
4. Press firmly for airtight seal
5. Drop sealed package into frame rabbet
6. Secure with glazier points

**Method 2: Kraft Paper Dust Cover (Traditional)**
1. **Cut kraft paper** 1 inch larger than frame back
2. **Apply double-sided tape or glue** around frame back edge
3. **Stretch paper over back** and press onto adhesive
4. **Trim excess** with a sharp blade
5. **Mist lightly with water** - paper will shrink as it dries for a drum-tight fit

**For Maximum Protection: Use BOTH!**
- Seal the package with framing tape first
- Then add the kraft paper dust cover on the frame back
- This creates two barriers against moisture and contaminants

Benefits:
- Keeps out dust, insects, and moisture
- Creates a professional finished look
- Artwork stays pristine for decades

### Attaching Hanging Hardware

**For small frames (under 8x10):**
- Single sawtooth hanger centered on top

**For medium frames:**
- Two D-rings with picture wire
- Mount D-rings ⅓ down from top on each side
- String wire between, leaving slight slack

**For heavy frames:**
- Two D-rings with heavy-duty wire
- Or two hooks requiring two wall nails

## Part 5: Fundraiser-Specific Tips

### Batch Production Strategy

1. **Standardize sizes** - Pick 2-3 common sizes (5x7, 8x10, 11x14)
2. **Cut all wood at once** - Assembly line style
3. **Glue and clamp in batches** - Rotate through stations
4. **Stain/paint in batches** - Same color for 10+ frames at once

### Cost Reduction Tips

1. Buy wood in **bulk** from lumber yards, not craft stores
2. Use **reclaimed wood** (pallets, old furniture) for rustic look
3. Buy **glass at a glass shop** - cheaper than pre-cut
4. Purchase **mat board scraps** from frame shops
5. Skip mats on simple pieces to reduce cost

### Quality Control Checklist

Before selling, verify each frame:
- [ ] Corners tight with no gaps
- [ ] Finish smooth and even
- [ ] Glass clean and scratch-free
- [ ] Backing sealed properly
- [ ] Hanging hardware secure
- [ ] No glue residue visible

### Pricing Your Frames

**Cost-plus method:**
1. Calculate material cost per frame
2. Add labor estimate ($10-15/hour divided by frames made per hour)
3. Add 50-100% markup for fundraiser profit

**Example:**
- Materials: $8
- Labor (15 min at $12/hr): $3
- Subtotal: $11
- Sell for: $20-25

## Part 6: Safety Notes

⚠️ **Power Tool Safety:**
- Always wear safety glasses
- Use push sticks with table saw
- Clamp work when routing
- Never reach over spinning blades

⚠️ **Chemical Safety:**
- Work in ventilated area when staining/finishing
- Wear gloves with stains and finishes
- Properly dispose of oil-soaked rags (fire hazard!)

⚠️ **Glass Safety:**
- Wear gloves when handling glass
- Dispose of broken glass carefully
- Consider acrylic for frames kids will handle
